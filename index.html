Preface

Calculus made sense to me in a way that most mathematics did not. Not because it was easy — it was not — but because it described things that changed. Velocity and acceleration. The area under a curve. The box dimensions that maximize volume while minimizing material. I remember thinking: if I can write an equation for a falling object, I should be able to write one for sales figures, or service orders, or inventory levels at my family’s company.

I could not. I did not know where to start.

The equations we worked with in calculus had tidy, well-behaved variables. The variables I cared about — orders, failures, costs — did not come pre-packaged as functions. I did not know how to turn a spreadsheet into mathematics. I took a course that covered regression and did not recognize it as the tool I had been looking for. I finished my undergraduate degree in financial engineering with the vague sense that the mathematics I had learned was somehow adjacent to the problems I wanted to solve, but not quite touching them.

That gap stayed with me for a long time.

I went to graduate school in economics knowing almost nothing about the field. What drew me in was the mathematics — more of it, and different from what I had seen in financial engineering. I had always found it easy to develop an interest in new areas when the underlying structure was rigorous, and economics turned out to have plenty of that. I started working seriously with data — analysis, visualization, pattern recognition. The original question, the one about describing real business variables with equations, receded into the background. It felt like a different project.

It was not until my PhD that I realized I had been circling the same problem the entire time. Optimization, it turns out, is precisely the discipline of writing equations that describe what we want and finding the inputs that satisfy them. Machine learning is, at its core, the application of that idea to data. The regression I had not recognized as useful in my undergraduate years was the same regression I was now extending, formalizing, and building new variants of for my dissertation.

This book is my attempt to build that connection explicitly — to draw the straight line between calculus and ML that took me twenty years to see.

The mathematics here start from first principles and build toward the contributions of my PhD thesis: a family of Support Vector Regression models that optimize percentage-error loss functions directly. The early chapters require nothing beyond multivariable calculus. The later chapters are graduate-level, but the goal throughout is the same: to show where the equations come from and why they take the form they do.

Code examples appear in paired R and Python tabs. Advanced derivations are in collapsible sections — they are there for completeness, but skipping them does not break the argument.
