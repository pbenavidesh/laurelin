2 From Spreadsheet to Function

What do you do when a spreadsheet is no longer enough?

Not because the data is too large. Not because the formulas are too slow. Because the question you are asking cannot be answered with a formula you can write by hand — and you know it.

This is where most data work actually starts. Not with a model, not with an algorithm. With a question that a pivot table cannot answer, and the uncomfortable realization that something more systematic is needed.

This book is about building that something from scratch.

2.1 A company’s electricity bill, by the half-hour

Consider a company that operates in Victoria, Australia. Its infrastructure runs on electricity — servers, cooling systems, office equipment. Every half-hour, the grid records how much power the company draws. Every half-hour, the wholesale price of that electricity changes based on how much everyone else is drawing at the same time.

If you can predict demand before it happens, you can act: pre-scale servers, shift workloads, negotiate contracts. If you cannot, you react — and reacting is expensive.

The prediction problem here is different in character from the electricity problem. There is no time axis. Each observation is independent — knowing the house value in Sacramento tells you nothing about the next row. What matters is the relationship between the eight features and the target. And that relationship is not linear. Census blocks near the coast behave differently from inland blocks even when income is held fixed. Again: a formula you can write by hand will not capture this. You need a function that learns the pattern from the data.

2.3 What functions do that equations do not

When you write a formula in a spreadsheet — say, =0.5 * B2 + 1200 — you are making a decision. You chose the slope. You chose the intercept. The formula does exactly what you told it to do, no more.

That is fine when the relationship is simple and known in advance. It fails when the relationship is complex, nonlinear, and discovered only by looking at the data.

This book is about the transition from formulas you write to functions that are fitted — determined by an optimization problem rather than by hand. The core idea is simple, even if the machinery is not: you describe what a good prediction looks like (a loss function), and you search for the function that makes predictions as good as possible (optimization).

Everything else — kernels, dual problems, support vectors — is in service of this idea.

2.4 Two problems, one framework

These two datasets are not chosen at random. They represent the two main structural types of prediction problem that appear in practice.

The electricity data is a time series: observations are ordered in time, and the recent past matters. Predicting tomorrow’s demand depends on what happened yesterday. Patterns repeat weekly and annually. The right model must respect time.

The California data is cross-sectional: observations are exchangeable, and there is no natural ordering. What matters is the geometry of the feature space — which inputs are similar, and whether similar inputs produce similar outputs.

The models developed in this book handle both. The optimization framework is the same. The loss function adapts to the measurement scale. The kernel decides what “similar” means.

By Part IV, you will have the tools to fit these datasets with SVR models that optimize percentage error directly — a choice that matters when the target varies over several orders of magnitude, as house prices and electricity demand both do.

For now, look at the data. Ask what it would mean to predict it well. The answer to that question will motivate every chapter that follows.

NoteExercise 1.1 — Reading the time series

Load vic_elec and compute the average half-hourly demand separately for weekdays and weekends. Plot both on the same axis. At what time of day is the gap between weekday and weekend demand largest?

What you need: dplyr, lubridate, ggplot2.

NoteExercise 1.2 — The price of a bad prediction

Suppose electricity costs $150 per MWh during peak hours (5–9 PM weekdays) and $40 per MWh otherwise. Using the vic_elec data:

  1. Compute the total cost if you always procure exactly what was demanded.
  2. Compute the cost if your forecast is systematically 10% high during peak hours (you over-procure and the excess is wasted at zero value).
  3. Compute the cost if your forecast is 10% low during peak hours (you under-procure and must buy the deficit at a spot premium of $500/MWh).

Which direction of error is more expensive? Does this change how you would define “a good prediction”?

NoteExercise 1.3 — Scale and error metrics

For the California Housing data, compute three prediction errors for a naive model that predicts the training mean for every observation:

  • MAE — mean absolute error (in dollars)
  • RMSE — root mean square error (in dollars)
  • MAPE — mean absolute percentage error (unitless)

Now repeat for a model that always predicts $200,000.

Which error metric is most informative? Does a $50,000 error mean the same thing for a $100,000 house as for a $600,000 house?

This question motivates Chapter 16.

NoteExercise 1.4 — Graduate level: loss functions as design choices

The choice of loss function is a statement about what errors matter.

Consider a delivery company predicting package transit times. A 2-hour error on a 4-hour delivery is qualitatively different from a 2-hour error on a 48-hour delivery — yet mean squared error treats them identically.

Formalize this argument. Define a loss function \(\ell(\hat{y}, y)\) that satisfies: (a) it is zero when prediction is perfect, (b) it is scale-free (invariant to multiplying both \(\hat{y}\) and \(y\) by a positive constant), and (c) it penalizes over-prediction and under-prediction symmetrically.

Is there more than one such function? What structural property do they share?
