1  The Art of Being Systematically Less Wrong

  • Show All Code
  • Hide All Code
  • View Source

There are two kinds of forecasters: those who can’t predict the future, and those who don’t know they can’t predict the future.

The first group has an advantage. They stop trying to be right and start trying to be less wrong — and that turns out to be a problem you can actually solve.

1.1 A decision you make every day

You are running out of coffee at home. You need to decide how many bags to buy. Buy too few and you run out before the week ends. Buy too many and they go stale before you finish them. Neither is catastrophic, but both have a cost.

Without thinking about it, you are solving an optimization problem. You have a loss — the combined cost of under-buying and over-buying — and you are trying to minimize it given what you know: your consumption rate, how long bags last, how often you go to the store.

Now scale that up. You are not buying coffee for one person. You are managing the electricity grid for an entire region. Every thirty minutes, you need to decide how much power to procure for the next interval. Buy too little and you impose rolling blackouts. Buy too much and you pay for energy that goes nowhere. The cost of being wrong is not stale coffee — it is millions of dollars and compromised infrastructure.

The structure of the problem is the same. The stakes are not.

1.2 Two datasets, one question

This book uses two datasets as its main thread. They come from different domains and have different structures, but they share the same underlying question: given what you can observe, what is your best prediction of what you cannot?

1.2.1 Victorian electricity demand

vic_elec records half-hourly electricity demand and temperature across the state of Victoria, Australia, from 2012 to 2014. Every row is a moment. Every moment is a decision someone had to make about how much power to have available.

Code 
library(tsibbledata)
library(dplyr)
library(ggplot2)
library(patchwork)
library(here)

vic_elec <- readr::read_csv(here("data", "vic_elec.csv"),
                             show_col_types = FALSE) |>
  mutate(Time = as.POSIXct(Time))

vic_sample <- vic_elec |>
  filter(Time >= as.POSIXct("2014-01-01"),
         Time <  as.POSIXct("2014-01-15"))

p_demand <- ggplot(vic_sample, aes(x = Time, y = Demand)) +
  geom_line(colour = "#2166ac", linewidth = 0.5) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = "Demand (MW)") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11)

p_temp <- ggplot(vic_sample, aes(x = Time, y = Temperature)) +
  geom_line(colour = "#d6604d", linewidth = 0.5) +
  labs(x = NULL, y = "Temperature (°C)") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11)

p_demand / p_temp

Temperature drives demand — air conditioning is the dominant load in an Australian summer. But the relationship shifts. A hot Tuesday looks different from a hot Saturday. A heat wave on the third consecutive day draws more than a heat wave on the first. The signal is real; it is just not simple enough to write down by hand.

1.2.2 California housing prices

The second dataset is 20,640 California census blocks from the early 1990s. Each block is described by eight numbers: median income, house age, average rooms, average bedrooms, population, average occupancy, latitude, longitude. The target is median house value.

Code 
library(ggplot2)
library(here)

California <- readr::read_csv(here("data", "california_housing.csv"),
                               show_col_types = FALSE)

ggplot(California, aes(x = longitude, y = latitude,
                       colour = log(median_house_value))) +
  geom_point(size = 0.3, alpha = 0.5) +
  scale_colour_viridis_c(option = "plasma", name = "log(value)") +
  coord_fixed(ratio = 1.3) +
  labs(x = "Longitude", y = "Latitude") +
  theme_minimal(base_size = 11)

There is no time axis here. Census blocks do not arrive in sequence — each one is independent. What matters is not what happened before, but which other blocks are similar and whether similar blocks tend to have similar prices. The coastal premium is visible in the plot. So is the concentration of high-value blocks in the Bay Area and Los Angeles. But the relationship between the eight features and the target is not something you can read off a map.

1.3 Why a formula is not enough

When you write =0.3 * B2 + 85000 in a spreadsheet, you have already made the decision. You chose the slope. You chose the intercept. The formula does what you told it to do, nothing more.

For the electricity problem, you do not know the slope in advance. You do not even know that the relationship is linear. The only honest starting point is the data itself — and the question of what it means for a prediction to be good.

That question has a precise answer. You choose a way to measure how wrong a prediction is. Then you find the function that is least wrong, on average, over all the data you have. That search is optimization. Every model in this book is the result of that search, with different choices about what “wrong” means and what functions are allowed.

1.4 What comes next

Part I builds the optimization machinery from scratch. You will see how to find the minimum of a function when there are no constraints, then when there are. You will learn to read the conditions that tell you whether a solution is optimal. By the end, you will have the tools to derive models from first principles — not just use them.

Parts II and III introduce the models themselves: how to measure similarity between observations, and how to use that similarity to build predictions that generalize beyond the training data.

The final chapters return to these two datasets with models that optimize percentage error directly — a choice that matters when a $50,000 prediction error means something very different for a $100,000 house than for a $600,000 house.

1.5 Exercises

NoteExercise 1.1 — Reading the time series

Load vic_elec and compute the average half-hourly demand separately for weekdays and weekends. Plot both on the same axis. At what hour of the day is the gap between weekday and weekend demand largest?

NoteExercise 1.2 — The cost of being wrong

Suppose electricity costs $150 per MWh during peak hours (5–9 PM on weekdays) and $40 per MWh at all other times. Using vic_elec:

  1. Compute the total procurement cost if you always buy exactly what was demanded.
  2. Compute the cost if your forecast is 10% high during peak hours (you over-procure; the excess is wasted).
  3. Compute the cost if your forecast is 10% low during peak hours (you under-procure; you must buy the deficit at a spot premium of $500 per MWh).

Which direction of error is more expensive? Does your answer change how you would define a good prediction?

NoteExercise 1.3 — What does “wrong” mean?

For the California Housing data, compute prediction errors for two naive models: one that always predicts the training mean, and one that always predicts $200,000.

Compute MAE, RMSE, and MAPE for each. Which metric best captures whether the model is useful? Does a $50,000 error mean the same thing for a $100,000 house and a $600,000 house?

NoteExercise 1.4

A loss function \(\ell(\hat{y}, y)\) is scale-free if multiplying both the prediction and the target by the same positive constant leaves the loss unchanged: \(\ell(c\hat{y}, cy) = \ell(\hat{y}, y)\) for all \(c > 0\).

Show that squared error \((\hat{y} - y)^2\) is not scale-free. Then construct a loss function that is. Is it unique? What structural property do all scale-free loss functions share?
